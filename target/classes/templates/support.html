Support

If you need a place to start

Some days you do not need a long list. You just need one clear next step.

Right Now

If the day feels like too much

Put down the thing that is making your chest tighten for five minutes. Sit down, drink some water, and let the pace drop a little.

Reach out to one steady person. A short message is enough: "I am having a rough day. Can you stay with me for a bit?"

If you feel unsafe or think you might hurt yourself, get to a trusted person nearby or contact local emergency services right away.

Who to reach

Start with the nearest steady person

For children: a parent, guardian, teacher, or school counselor.

For teens and students: a favorite teacher, counselor, mentor, sibling, roommate, or close friend.

For adults and older adults: a partner, family member, close friend, doctor, therapist, or community support person.

Children

Keep it simple and close to home.

Children usually need less analysis and more steadiness. Food, rest, routine, and one calm adult can do a lot.

If something feels off for more than a few days, involve a parent, guardian, teacher, or school counselor early.

Teens And Young Adults

Pressure builds quietly here.

School, exams, friendships, family expectations, and uncertainty about the future can stack up fast even when nothing looks dramatic from outside.

Try less isolation, more sleep, and one honest conversation with someone you trust before things pile up further.

Adults And Older Adults

Stress often hides inside responsibility.

Work, finances, caregiving, health changes, and family demands can make people look functional while feeling worn down underneath.

If that sounds familiar, aim for one practical relief point today: cancel one thing, ask for help with one thing, or book one real support conversation.

Small Steps

Small things that matter

Food, water, sleep, and a short walk sound basic, but they matter when everything feels loud.

One honest line in the journal counts. It does not need to be deep or well written.

Asking earlier is usually easier than waiting until everything crashes at once.

Use this space

How this app helps

Use the check-in when you know something feels off but you do not have the words yet.

Use the journal when the week is blurring together and you want a clearer record of how things have been.

If the app nudges you toward extra support, take that seriously and bring a real person into the picture.
